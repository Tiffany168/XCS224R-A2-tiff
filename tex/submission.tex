  The plot shows that the configuration with 10 critics and UTD=5 (cyan) reaches a perfect success rate of 1.0 significantly faster ($\sim$46k environment steps) compared to the baseline with 2 critics and UTD=1 (black), which reaches ${\gtrsim}\,0.9$ at 82k steps.

  \begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \toprule
    Configuration & Max Success Rate & Steps to $\geq$0.9 Success & Time (at 98k steps) \\
    \midrule
    Baseline (2 critics, UTD=1) & $\sim$0.97 & $\sim$82,000 & 8.14 min \\
    Optimized (10 critics, UTD=5) & 1.0 & $\sim$44,000 & 55.55 min \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Comparison of baseline and optimized configurations.}
  \end{table}

  \paragraph{Explanation of Effects}

  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sample efficiency vs.\ runtime.} UTD=5 performs five gradient steps per environment step, extracting more learning signal from each transition and converging in roughly half the environment steps. However, this comes at a steep computational cost: 25 minutes (44k steps, ${\gtrsim}\,0.9$
 episode success) versus 7 minutes (82k steps, ${\gtrsim}\,0.9$
 episode success). This reflects the known tension in high-UTD training: better sample efficiency at the cost of time.

    \item \textbf{High UTD causes instability, mitigated by larger ensemble.} As the assignment notes, too many gradient steps can make learning unstable by amplifying Q-value estimation errors. This is visible in the early volatility of the cyan curve and is confirmed by the significantly higher critic loss ($\sim$13.9 vs.\ $\sim$2.4 for the baseline). The 10-critic ensemble mitigates this by providing more conservative Bellman targets: taking the minimum of 2 critics sampled from a pool of 10 yields lower, less overestimated Q-values than sampling from only 2, preventing the actor from being misled by inflated Q-estimates despite the aggressive update schedule.
  \end{enumerate}
